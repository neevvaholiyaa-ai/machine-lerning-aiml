## Problem Statement

India's tourism sector generates data across multiple disconnected dimensions — annual foreign, NRI, and Indian tourist arrivals
, age-group and quarterly distribution patterns, India's global tourism ranking, region and purpose-wise visitor breakdown, 
monument-wise footfall, and state-wise domestic/foreign visitor statistics. 
This data is spread across separate government datasets, making it difficult to identify unified trends, seasonal patterns, 
and the impact of external disruptions like COVID-19 on India's tourism industry.

## Objective

This project performs an in-depth exploratory data analysis across 8 tourism datasets (spanning 1981–2021) to identify trends in tourist arrivals,
seasonal and demographic patterns, India's global tourism ranking, and the most popular destinations and monuments.
The goal is to convert raw historical tourism data into actionable insights that support data-driven decisions around marketing, 
infrastructure planning, and policy design. This analysis also lays the foundation for future time-series forecasting of tourist arrivals.

## Key Insights Summary

- Tourist arrivals in India (FTAs, NRIs, ITAs) showed steady growth from 2016–2019, followed by a sharp decline in 2020 due to COVID-19, confirming 2020 as an anomalous year across all metrics.
- The 25–54 age group forms the majority of tourist arrivals, making it the primary segment to target for growth strategies.
- Q4 (Oct–Dec) sees the highest tourist inflow, likely driven by the festive season, while Q3 (Jul–Sep) sees the lowest, likely due to the monsoon.
- India's global tourism rank was strongest between 2000–2015 but declined post-COVID.
- Western Europe is the leading source region (23.3% share); Bangladesh is consistently the top contributing country (2017–2019).
- Taj Mahal remains India's most visited monument, even during the pandemic period.
- Andhra Pradesh leads in domestic tourist visits; Delhi leads in foreign tourist visits.

  
## Business Recommendations

- Treat 2020 as an outlier year in any trend/forecasting analysis due to COVID-19's abnormal impact.
- Focus marketing and infrastructure investment on the 25–54 age group, the largest contributing segment.
- Launch targeted campaigns for the 15–24 age group through student travel programs and technical/educational tourism packages.
- Increase promotional activity around Q4 (festive season) and design offers to boost Q3 (monsoon) footfall through indoor/wellness tourism.
- Strengthen medical tourism infrastructure to attract more health-related travelers and build resilience against future disruptions.
- Prioritize marketing in Western Europe and Bangladesh, the strongest source markets, while developing strategies to grow underperforming regions like Australasia and North America.
- Leverage Taj Mahal's consistent popularity to cross-promote lesser-visited monuments and distribute tourist footfall more evenly.
- Support states with lower domestic/foreign visits by replicating strategies used in top-performing states like Andhra Pradesh and Delhi.

**Future Scope:** This analysis will be extended with time-series forecasting models to predict future tourist arrivals and support proactive planning.
